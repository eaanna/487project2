Where do the bears live?

This map, created from three seperate maps for each species found in North America, shows the current distribution of bears across the continent. Historically, bear populations have been shrinking, including the endangered Polar Bear and the Grizzly Bear. However, Black and Brown Bears as a whole have made a comeback within recent decades.

Out of the near million bears that inhabit North America, the vast majority are Black Bears. While both Black and Brown Bears have made a comeback in populations and have no vulnerability concerns, Brown Bears stagger behind as a whole. Only over 1 percent of bears in North America are Polar Bears. However, it is noted by biologists around the world that populations of endangered species, such as Polar Bears, are difficult to measure.

Be Bear-y Aware

Bear management is one of the most important aspects of saving the bear populations in North America. American national groups, such as the National Park Service or the US Fish and Wildlife Service, emphasize how important it is to be educated about the furry creatures. Education about bear conservation and prevention of human-bear interaction is essential to future bear survival in North America.

In a response to bears digging through trash, parks have implemented "bear proof" trashcans to stop bears from becoming comfortable with and associating humans with food.

Aside from education, wildlife groups use various methods to stop bears from interacting with humans. One major method is negative reinforcement during bear encounters. Black and Brown Bears are omnivores, and are not picky, choosing to eat anything that smells good and gives them energy. As a result, when habitats shrink or a bear wanders too far, human trash is appealing. In an encounter with a bear in backyards, it is good practice to make loud noise and scare the bear off. Scaring the bear increases the changes of the bear seeing humans negatively and not returning.

How does North America stand?

Compared to other continents, bears are widely known about and protected by governments in North America. Black and Brown Bears are on the rise, and have seen the greatest efforts in North America. Organizations such as the World Wildlife Fund, Free the Bears, and the International Bear Association work to conserve bear populations globally. Some of the efforts include education and rescue from Asian bile farms and circuses where many bears are exploited.

The International Union for the Conservation of Nature periodically releases a 'red list' that has the endangerment levels of plant and animal species across the world. Out of eight bear species, only two are of least concern, the Black and Brown bear. However, although these bears are of least concern, they may be endangered or extinct in some local areas, or may have extinct subspecies. The other six species are considered vulnerable.

What if I encounter a bear?

According to an article from Vox Media, only 71 people have been killed in bear attacks in wilderness areas in the United States, compared to a total of 158 in North America since 1900. For the millions of people who explore nature in North America have a minimal chance of encountering a bear attack, much less a fatal one. In general, bears will either charge as a bluff or in aggression. A bluff is typically indicated by the bear with its head up and body puffed to appear bigger. The bear may charge by running fast in big leaps then veer in a different direction as it approaches. In an attack, however, the bear will yawn, clack their teeth, stomp their paws on the ground and huff, and their head and ears will be down.

In the event that you see a bear around, here are some tips to keep you safe. (Black and Brown Bears)

During a Bluff:

During a Bluff: slowly back away, raise your arms, and speak to show the bear you are a human. Do not run or continue moving if the bear begins to charge. Move away as soon as the bear stops moving.

In an Attack:

Brown Bears: PLAY DEAD. Proper form includes laying on the stomach, legs spread, and hands covering the neck and head with the arms. Keep equipment on to keep from more damage. Stay still until the bear leaves and for several minutes after to ensure the bear leaves the area. If the bear persists (a last resort): fight back with any means possible.

Black Bears: FIGHT. Aim for the face, such as the nose and eyes. Use any resource as a weapon, such as rocks.

*Note: Polar Bear interactions are extremely rare and limited, and they are known carnivores. Those who live in the Arctic Circle and surrounding areas are likely to take preventative measures to avoid Polar Bears completely.
